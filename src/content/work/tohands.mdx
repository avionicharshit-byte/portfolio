## TL;DR

Owned the product surface of a smart POS calculator from 0 → 6,000+ devices in 14 months. It's now the company's primary revenue line. The non-obvious call: treating the factory diagnostic pipeline as a product problem, not an ops problem.

---

## Context

Small merchants in India manage inventory and checkout with a mix of paper ledgers, WhatsApp voice notes, and whatever POS system a distributor sold them. The pain isn't missing features — it's speed and reliability at the counter, with a customer queue behind them. Tohands built a smart POS calculator targeting this gap. I owned the product surface from the first firmware build to scale.

## The calls I made

### 1. Prioritized firmware features from on-ground merchant pain, not feature requests

Translated store visits and call logs into a 6-week roadmap with the founders. Tradeoff: shipped fewer, higher-signal features versus a long backlog. The result was features merchants actually used versus ones that looked impressive in a pitch.

### 2. Optimized for perceived checkout speed

Sub-second SKU search across 15,000+ products. Lifted POS session completion tracked in Mixpanel. Tradeoff: indexing and memory cost on constrained hardware versus merchant-felt speed. On a device with tight constraints, every allocation is a product decision.

### 3. Treated the factory as part of the product

Built a 9-test automated diagnostic suite that ran on every unit before it left the floor. First-pass yield improved to 90%. Per-device test time dropped 60%. I framed the factory test pipeline as a UX problem — the cost of a defective device reaching a merchant is an NPS disaster, not an ops line item.
